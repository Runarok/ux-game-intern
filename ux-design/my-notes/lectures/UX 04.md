# 1. UX Research – Definition
UX research is the process of **collecting insights about users, their needs, behaviors, and environments** to design better digital products.

Research focuses on understanding:
- who the users are
- what goals they have
- what features they value
- how they use products
- what problems or pain points they face

Goal:
Design products that are **useful, usable, and aligned with real user needs**.

---

# 2. What UX Research Investigates
UX research gathers insights about several aspects:

### Users
- current users
- potential users
- future users

### User needs and goals
- tasks users want to complete
- motivations behind product usage
- desired features and content

### User pain points
- problems faced with current products
- usability issues
- friction points during tasks

### Context of use
- where the product is used
- when it is used
- devices used (mobile, desktop, etc.)

### User skill level
- technical knowledge
- experience with similar products
- domain expertise

### Personalization
- tailoring design and functionality to user preferences.

---

# 3. Competitive and Market Research
UX research also examines the broader market.

Key areas:
- competitor products
- industry trends
- new technologies
- feature comparisons

Purpose:
- identify gaps in the market
- differentiate the product
- maintain competitive advantage.

---

# 4. Importance of UX Research
UX research is critical for product success.

Benefits include:

### Better product usability
Understanding user behavior allows designers to create **simpler and more effective interfaces**.

### Increased revenue and market share
Products that solve real user problems attract more users.

### Reduced development cost
Research helps focus development on **high-value features** rather than unnecessary ones.

### Better decision making
Research provides **data-driven insights** instead of relying on assumptions or internal debates.

### Competitive advantage
Understanding trends and competitor weaknesses helps companies act earlier.

---

# 5. Role of UX Designers in Research
UX designers often participate directly in research.

Responsibilities may include:
- conducting research
- supervising researchers
- validating research quality
- implementing research insights into designs.

Direct interaction with users helps designers build **better design intuition**.

---

# 6. Types of UX Research
There are two main types of UX research.

---

## 6.1 Foundational (Generative) Research
Conducted **early in the product lifecycle**.

Purpose:
- understand users
- identify opportunities
- discover pain points
- shape product strategy.

Common methods:
- interviews
- surveys
- focus groups
- ethnography
- market research.

It supports **long-term product strategy**.

---

## 6.2 Evaluative (Experimental) Research
Conducted **after a prototype or product exists**.

Purpose:
- evaluate usability
- optimize existing designs
- test product improvements.

Common methods:
- user testing
- A/B testing
- first-click testing
- usability evaluation.

It supports **short-term improvements**.

---

# 7. Importance of User Testing
User testing is one of the most valuable UX research methods.

Key insight:
- Around **85% of usability issues can be identified through testing**.

Despite its importance, only **about 55% of companies conduct user testing**.

User testing helps detect:
- usability problems
- confusing navigation
- feature misunderstandings
- unmet user expectations.

---

# 8. User Recruitment for UX Research
Recruiting participants is called **user recruitment**.

Participants must represent **real target users**.

They should match characteristics defined in **user personas**.

Possible characteristics include:
- demographics
- product usage habits
- profession
- experience level
- knowledge domain
- personality traits
- device usage
- buying behavior.

---

# 9. Sources for Recruiting Participants
There are several recruitment approaches.

### Personal networks
- colleagues
- friends
- family.

Fast but may not represent real users.

---

### Recruitment agencies
Professional firms recruit participants matching specific criteria.

Pros:
- efficient
- structured process.

Cons:
- expensive
- quality varies.

---

### Online research platforms
Examples include specialized UX research platforms.

Users sign up to participate in research for compensation.

Advantages:
- quick recruitment
- remote testing.

Limitation:
- difficult for niche target groups.

---

### Advertising
Participants can be recruited through:

- social media ads
- classified ads
- community posts.

Ads should include:
- participation criteria
- research description
- time requirement
- incentives.

Avoid revealing product details.

---

### Social media communities
UX researchers can recruit participants from relevant online groups.

Benefits:
- access to niche audiences
- community engagement.

---

### Special interest groups
Organizations or communities related to the product domain.

Examples:
- professional associations
- hobby groups
- industry communities.

---

### Public place recruitment
Participants are approached in places where target users are likely to be found.

This is called **guerrilla research**.

Suitable for:
- interviews
- surveys
- quick usability tests.

---

### Research participant panels
A database of people who agreed to participate in future research.

Sources:
- website opt-in forms
- previous research participants
- mailing lists.

---

### Snowball recruitment
Existing participants recommend other suitable participants.

Advantages:
- quick
- low cost.

---

# 10. Participant Screening
Before confirming participation, researchers must verify that the participant matches selection criteria.

Screening may involve:
- short interviews
- phone calls
- questionnaires.

The goal is **ensuring participant suitability**.

Product details should usually **not be revealed during screening**.

---

# 11. Incentives for Participants
Participants are often compensated.

Possible incentives:
- money
- gift vouchers
- products
- refreshments
- travel reimbursement.

Compensation encourages participation and respects the participant’s time.

---

# 12. Definition of User Testing
User testing is a UX research method where:

- users interact with a product or prototype
- researchers observe their behavior
- feedback is collected.

Important principle:
The **product is tested**, not the user.

If users struggle, the product design must improve.

---

# 13. Types of User Testing

### Moderated testing
A researcher guides the session.

Features:
- direct interaction
- probing questions
- observation of user behavior.

Most common approach.

---

### Unmoderated testing
Automated testing conducted through software platforms.

Features:
- users follow automated instructions
- interaction data is recorded automatically.

Used for large-scale testing.

---

# 14. Testing Environment
Ideally testing should occur in the **real environment where the product is used**.

Example:
Testing a fitness app in a gym.

However, testing can also occur in:
- meeting rooms
- offices
- remote sessions.

Usability labs are not always necessary.

---

# 15. User Test Tasks
Participants perform tasks using the product.

Examples:
- creating an account
- searching for content
- configuring settings
- completing workflows.

Task design principles:

### Prioritize tasks
Start with the most important tasks.

### Start broad, then specific
Observe natural navigation before testing specific features.

### Follow natural workflow
Tasks should mirror real-life usage sequences.

### Begin with simple tasks
Build user confidence before introducing complex tasks.

### Keep tasks realistic
Use real-world scenarios.

---

# 16. Writing Good Test Tasks
Tasks should be **general and scenario-based**, not instruction-based.

Example:

Poor task:
“Go to settings and disable notifications.”

Better task:
“Show how you would stop receiving notifications.”

This prevents giving users clues about navigation paths.

---

# 17. User Testing Moderation Process

## Step 1 — Brief the research team
Explain:
- research objectives
- roles of moderators and observers
- observation rules.

Observers should **not interact with participants** during testing.

---

## Step 2 — Welcome and brief the participant
Actions:
- greet participant
- introduce research team
- explain testing process
- emphasize that the product is being tested.

Participants should:
- think aloud during tasks
- feel free to stop tasks anytime.

Consent forms are usually required.

---

## Step 3 — Moderation during testing
Moderator responsibilities:

- assign tasks
- encourage thinking aloud
- ask probing questions
- observe behavior
- record observations.

Important observations:
- hesitation
- confusion
- frustration
- unexpected navigation.

If a participant becomes stuck, the moderator may assist to continue testing other tasks.

---

## Step 4 — Post-test questions
After tasks are completed, ask participants:

- what they liked
- what they disliked
- suggestions for improvement.

Common metric:
Net Promoter Score (NPS)

Example question:
“How likely are you to recommend this product to others?”

---

## Step 5 — Debrief the participant
Actions:
- thank the participant
- provide incentive
- ask if they are willing to participate in future studies.

Participants may be added to a research panel.

---

## Step 6 — Debrief the research team
Immediately after the session:

- summarize observations
- capture insights
- record usability problems.

Delaying this step may cause important insights to be forgotten.

---

# 18. Key Takeaways
Important concepts to remember:

- UX research gathers insights about users, needs, and behaviors.
- Research aligns product design with **real user needs**.
- Two main research types exist: **generative research and evaluative research**.
- User testing is the most common UX research technique.
- Recruiting the right participants is critical to valid research.
- Participants must represent real target users.
- UX testing focuses on evaluating the **product**, not judging the user.
- Effective test design includes realistic tasks and careful observation.
- Moderated sessions involve briefing, testing, feedback, and debriefing.
- Immediate documentation of findings ensures valuable insights are preserved.
